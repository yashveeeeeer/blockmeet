Why LPG Rationing Will Break the System It Claims to Save

An economic analysis of India's proposed LPG rationing policy
March 2026

India's LPG supply chain serves 33 crore households. It is run by three state-owned oil marketing companies, backed by over 25,000 distributors, and extends into the remotest districts of the country. When the government proposed rationing LPG cylinders to manage rising costs, it reached for the simplest available lever. But simplicity is not the same thing as effectiveness. Rationing does not lower costs. It restricts supply — and risks destabilising the only infrastructure capable of delivering clean cooking fuel at national scale.

What is rationing?

A government-imposed cap on the number of LPG cylinders per household per year. It restricts demand but does nothing about the cost of sourcing, refining, or delivering gas.

The Price Problem

Prices Rose 56%. The Cuts Were Political.

Between 2019 and 2022, the price of a domestic LPG cylinder in Delhi climbed from ₹706 to ₹1,053. Then the government intervened — twice. A ₹200 cut in August 2023. Another ₹100 in March 2024. Both times, directly before elections. Both times, the cost difference was absorbed not by the treasury but by the oil marketing companies themselves.

The pattern is unmistakable: price hikes are gradual and market-driven. Cuts are sudden, political, and unfunded.

Nearly half the distribution network is rural. Eight percent operates in terrain the government officially classifies as “difficult” — hilly, remote, hard to reach. No private competitor serves these routes. They exist because state-owned companies are mandated to go where the market will not.

Ujjwala — the government’s flagship clean-cooking programme, 10.35 crore connections — runs entirely on this infrastructure. Rationing threatens the very network it depends on.

The Fiscal Reality

₹53,700 Crore in Losses. And Counting.

 ₹53,700 Cr  — that is the cumulative under-recovery from selling LPG below cost. The government has compensated about ₹30,000 crore. The remaining ₹23,700 crore sits on the books of three public-sector companies as uncompensated loss.

₹23,700 Cr gap — absorbed by state-owned companies, not the treasury.

Here is what makes this structural, not cyclical: 60% of India’s LPG is imported. It is purchased in dollars at international prices and sold in rupees at controlled domestic prices. That gap does not close with rationing — it widens, because fixed costs remain constant while volumes drop.

Equity vs. Efficiency

Who Wins, Who Loses — and the Equity Paradox

Rationing sounds equitable: everyone gets the same cap. In practice, a family of three in Bandra and a joint family of nine in Bundelkhand both receive the same quota. One is comfortable. The other goes back to firewood. That is not equity — it is uniformity masquerading as fairness.

Logical Prior is the metric that settles this debate. No stakeholder receives LPG if the supply chain cannot deliver it. Supplier viability is not one interest among many — it is a precondition for every other stakeholder’s benefit. Scale: 33 crore households, every district. Urgency: ₹53,700 crore in accumulated losses, growing every quarter.

The Way Forward

Three Reforms Instead of Rationing

The cost problem is real. Doing nothing is not an option. But the solution is structural reform, not supply restriction. Three measures — each implementable within existing institutional frameworks — would address the root causes that rationing merely papers over.

Targeted DBT, Not Blanket Rationing

Subsidy reaches BPL and Ujjwala households directly via existing Direct Benefit Transfer infrastructure. Everyone else pays a fair, cost-recovery price. The vulnerable are still protected. The supply chain stops haemorrhaging.

Deregulate Commercial LPG

Hotels, restaurants, and industry can absorb market rates. They already do in most comparable economies. This alone eliminates a substantial portion of under-recoveries without affecting a single household.

Quarterly Price Indexing

A transparent formula tied to the Saudi Contract Price, forex, and a reasonable margin — reviewed by PPAC, with a cap on per-quarter increases. Predictable for consumers. Sustainable for suppliers.

“The LPG supply chain keeps gas flowing to 33 crore homes. The question is not whether to fix the economics — it is whether to fix them intelligently, or break the system trying.”
